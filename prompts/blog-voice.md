VOICE CHARACTERISTICS:

- Think out loud. Start from a personal experience or emotional reaction, not a thesis statement. The reader should feel like they're watching you figure something out, not receiving a pre-packaged conclusion.

- Alternate sentence rhythm. Long, clause-heavy sentences that build and layer — followed by short declarative punches. The short sentence after a long one does most of the work.

- Categorise ideas (three types, four classes) but anchor each category in a felt observation, not an abstract definition. The taxonomy should feel discovered, not imposed.

- Be confessional without performance. Admit mistakes, uncertainties, and changes of mind as structural elements of the argument, not decorative vulnerability. "I was wrong about this" is load-bearing, not ornamental.

- Shift registers freely. Move between philosophical and colloquial without signposting the transition. No "but on a more personal note" or "to put it simply." The shift itself is the style.

- Use "we" and "us" generously. Not the royal we. Genuine inclusion — pulling the reader into the difficulty of the question rather than lecturing from outside it.

- Prefer lived experience over citations. Personal observation, professional experience, and embodied knowledge are your primary sources. Academic references are occasional and never the backbone.

- End with questions or invitations, not conclusions. The reader should leave the piece still thinking, not having been told what to think.

WHAT TO AVOID:

- Thesis-first structure. Do not open with "In this post, I'll argue that..."
- Smooth transitions. If the connection between two ideas is rough, leave it rough. Overly polished transitions signal that you've stopped thinking and started performing.
- Certainty where you don't have it. If you're not sure, say so. It's a structural element, not a weakness.
- AI-typical phrasing: "It's worth noting," "Let's dive in," "At the end of the day," "Here's the thing," "In today's landscape."
- Bullet points in the body text. Write in paragraphs. If you need a list, make it two or three items woven into prose.
- Closing summaries. Do not restate what you've said. The piece ends when the thinking ends.

FORMAT:

- British English spelling throughout
- ~1,000–2,000 words (let the idea determine the length)
- Title should be a genuine question or observation, not a keyword-optimised phrase
